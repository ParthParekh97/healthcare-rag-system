Title: Evidence-Based Hypertension Management Guidelines
Source: American Heart Association / American College of Cardiology (AHA/ACC)
Document ID: HC-002

1. Definition and Classification

Hypertension is defined as persistently elevated arterial blood pressure. The AHA/ACC 2017 guidelines classify blood pressure as follows:
- Normal: Systolic < 120 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Elevated: Systolic 120-129 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: Systolic 130-139 mmHg OR Diastolic 80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: Systolic >= 140 mmHg OR Diastolic >= 90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: Systolic > 180 mmHg AND/OR Diastolic > 120 mmHg

2. Epidemiology

Hypertension affects approximately 47% of adults in the United States (116 million people). It is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and stroke. Prevalence increases with age, affecting over 70% of adults aged 65 and older. African Americans have the highest prevalence of hypertension globally.

3. Pathophysiology

Essential (primary) hypertension accounts for 90-95% of cases and is multifactorial. Contributing mechanisms include:
- Increased sympathetic nervous system activity
- Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) overactivation
- Endothelial dysfunction with reduced nitric oxide availability
- Increased peripheral vascular resistance
- Sodium retention and volume expansion
- Genetic predisposition (estimated 30-50% heritability)

Secondary hypertension (5-10% of cases) may result from renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, pheochromocytoma, Cushing syndrome, thyroid disorders, obstructive sleep apnea, or coarctation of the aorta.

4. Diagnostic Workup

Initial evaluation includes:
- Office blood pressure measurement using proper technique (patient seated, arm supported, appropriate cuff size)
- Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring (ABPM) or Home Blood Pressure Monitoring (HBPM) to confirm diagnosis
- Basic laboratory: Complete metabolic panel, complete blood count, lipid panel, thyroid-stimulating hormone, urinalysis, electrocardiogram
- Assessment for target organ damage: Echocardiography, fundoscopic examination, urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio

5. Treatment Targets

- General population: < 130/80 mmHg
- Patients with diabetes: < 130/80 mmHg
- Patients with chronic kidney disease: < 130/80 mmHg
- Elderly patients (>= 65 years): Systolic < 130 mmHg if tolerated; individualize based on frailty and comorbidities
- Pregnancy: < 140/90 mmHg (or < 135/85 mmHg per some guidelines)

6. First-Line Pharmacological Agents

Four classes of antihypertensive medications are recommended as first-line therapy:

a) ACE Inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril):
   - Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II
   - Preferred in: Diabetes, chronic kidney disease, heart failure, post-MI
   - Key side effects: Dry cough (10-15%), hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare)

b) Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs) (e.g., losartan, valsartan, olmesartan):
   - Mechanism: Block AT1 receptors
   - Preferred in: Same indications as ACE inhibitors; alternative when ACE inhibitor cough occurs
   - Key side effects: Hyperkalemia, rarely angioedema

c) Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs) (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine):
   - Mechanism: Block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle
   - Preferred in: African American patients, elderly, isolated systolic hypertension
   - Key side effects: Peripheral edema, headache, flushing

d) Thiazide Diuretics (e.g., chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide):
   - Mechanism: Inhibit sodium-chloride cotransporter in distal tubule
   - Preferred in: Elderly, African American patients, osteoporosis
   - Key side effects: Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia

7. Lifestyle Modifications for Hypertension

- DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension): Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; reduces BP by 8-14 mmHg
- Sodium restriction: < 2,300 mg/day (ideally < 1,500 mg/day); reduces BP by 5-6 mmHg
- Physical activity: 90-150 minutes per week of aerobic exercise; reduces BP by 5-8 mmHg
- Weight loss: Each kg lost reduces BP by approximately 1 mmHg
- Alcohol moderation: <= 2 drinks/day for men, <= 1 drink/day for women; reduces BP by 2-4 mmHg
- Smoking cessation: Does not directly lower BP but significantly reduces cardiovascular risk
- Stress management: Techniques such as meditation and cognitive behavioral therapy may provide modest BP reductions

8. Resistant Hypertension

Defined as blood pressure remaining above goal despite use of 3 optimally dosed antihypertensive agents of different classes, including a diuretic. Affects approximately 10-15% of treated hypertensive patients.
Evaluation should include assessment for medication adherence, white-coat effect, secondary causes, and interfering substances (NSAIDs, oral contraceptives, decongestants). Addition of spironolactone (25-50 mg/day) is recommended as fourth-line therapy.
